My name is Kevin Federico. I am a theoretical physicist with interests broadly in the fields of quantum information, quantum gravity, and condensed matter physics. Currently I am a PhD student at CU Boulder working with Chris Akers. I completed my undergraduate degree at Lehigh University advised by Timm Wrase. I am curious about how we can use what we have learned in quantum gravity to talk about many-body quantum systems and vice versa. To this end, quantum information provides a unifying theoretical language and quantum computing promises an experimental framework. I am particularly excited about attempts to experimentally realize quantum gravity on quantum computers. I have a small interest as well in the history of science, specifically in the "scientific method(s)" of past scientists.

At the moment, I find myself thinking about:

  • What role does complexity play in effective theories?
  • What are the details of holographic maps? Are such maps unique to holography or more general in nature?
  • Are there many-body systems which exhibit an emergent spacetime dimension? What would signatures of this look like experimentally?
  • How did early modern astronomers conduct their work? Can we use modern techniques to analyze old manuscripts?
  • What is Warhol's glue trap?
  • Why do mathematicians never come to blows? Why do philosophers?

In the past I have worked on:

  • The absence of localized intersecting sources in supergravity
  • Can we use black hole explosions as laboratories for new physics?
Outside of physics I find a life sustained by climbing, music, poetry, philosophy, fermentation, etc etc, etc. I am a generalist to first order and--like Wittgenstein--am by nature a hedgehog but am slowly becoming a fox.
